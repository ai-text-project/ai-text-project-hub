# Diagram Generation Workflow

## Purpose

This document records a reusable workflow for generating conceptual diagrams through structural interpretation rather than direct illustration.

The objective is not merely to create visual artifacts, but to preserve the relationship between:

* source text
* conceptual structure
* visual architecture
* external projection

This workflow emerged through repeated interactions involving article writing, diagram design, ASCII prototyping, and iterative refinement.

---

# Conceptual Foundation

## From Text to Structure

Traditional diagram production often follows:

```text
Text
↓
Image
```

In contrast, the workflow described here follows:

```text
Text
↓
Structural Interpretation
↓
Visual Skeleton
↓
Diagram
```

The diagram is therefore treated as an external projection of an internal conceptual structure.

---

## Compression vs. Recomposition

Two different operations should be distinguished.

### Compression

Reduction of visual density while preserving the original structure.

Example:

```text
Detailed Diagram
↓
Simplified Diagram
```

The structure remains largely unchanged.

---

### Recomposition

Transformation of visual form while preserving conceptual relationships.

Example:

```text
Layer Stack
↓
Triangular Interaction Model
```

The visual geometry changes, but the underlying conceptual structure remains stable.

---

## Diagram as Structural Projection

The purpose of a diagram is not merely explanation.

A diagram may function as:

* structural compression
* conceptual navigation
* orientation support
* externalized architecture

In this workflow, diagrams are regarded as projections of conceptual structures rather than illustrations of isolated ideas.

---

# Working Protocol

## Step 1 — Source Material

Prepare one of the following:

* article manuscript
* chapter draft
* research note
* architecture specification

The source material serves as the conceptual foundation.

---

## Step 2 — Structural Extraction

Identify:

* central concept
* hierarchy
* relationships
* directional flow
* major constraints

Example:

```text
Thought
↓
Structure
↓
AI Reads
↓
Response
```

The objective is to discover the structure behind the text.

---

## Step 3 — Design Specification

Create a diagram design document.

The specification should define:

* purpose
* intended audience
* diagram type
* layout principle
* visual emphasis
* information density

This stage stabilizes the intended structure before visual generation begins.

---

## Step 4 — ASCII Prototype

Construct a simplified visual skeleton.

Example:

```text
Phase Layer

     △

Structural    Literal

      ▼

Meaning Space
```

ASCII prototypes function as intermediate structural representations.

They help preserve conceptual relationships while avoiding premature visual decisions.

---

## Step 5 — Structural Review

Review the prototype for:

* conceptual clarity
* structural stability
* visual balance
* unnecessary complexity

Questions:

* What is the central idea?
* What can be removed?
* What must remain visible?
* Does the structure match the intended concept?

---

## Step 6 — Diagram Generation

Generate the visual diagram.

At this stage, the objective is not artistic decoration.

The objective is faithful external projection of the conceptual structure.

---

## Step 7 — Iterative Refinement

Possible refinements include:

### Compression

Reduce visual density.

### Recomposition

Change geometry while preserving structure.

### Reframing

Adjust title, emphasis, or visual focus.

---

## Step 8 — Design Notes

For important figures, preserve a short design note.

Suggested format:

```text
Original Concept:
Three-Layer Reading Model

Transformation:
Layer Stack
→ Interaction Geometry

Purpose:
To visualize simultaneous activation
rather than sequential processing.
```

This preserves the evolution path of the diagram.

Diagrams are not generated directly from text.
