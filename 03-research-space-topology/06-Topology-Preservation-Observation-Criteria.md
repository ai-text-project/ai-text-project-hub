## Purpose

This document provides the methodological foundation for evaluating whether
developmental topology is preserved across different observational contexts.

The purpose is not to define a procedural workflow.

Instead, this document establishes the conceptual criteria that guide structural
observation before protocol execution.

# Observation Philosophy

Structural observation does not begin by comparing semantic similarity.

Instead, observation begins by asking whether the underlying developmental
organization remains preserved despite differences in representation.

Accordingly,

- representation may change,
- terminology may change,
- visual appearance may change,

while developmental topology remains invariant.

The objective is therefore to observe structural preservation rather than semantic identity.

---

# Observation Principles

The following principles guide all subsequent structural observation.

## Principle 1

Observe structure before interpretation.

Interpretation should not precede structural identification.

---

## Principle 2

Compare topology before meaning.

Equivalent developmental organization may appear across different domains despite
semantic differences.

---

## Principle 3

Identify invariants before differences.

Observation first seeks stable structural relationships.

Differences are interpreted only after structural invariants have been identified.

---

## Principle 4

Preserve observational neutrality.

Criteria should describe observable organizational characteristics rather than
theoretical conclusions.

---

# Observation Criteria

Topology preservation is provisionally evaluated through repeated observation of
the following structural categories.

## Criterion 1 — Node

Identify major organizational components.

Examples include:

- conceptual units
- functional modules
- operational stages

---

## Criterion 2 — Relation

Observe how nodes are connected.

Typical relationships include:

- dependency
- correspondence
- interaction
- recursive linkage

---

## Criterion 3 — Flow

Observe developmental direction.

Examples include:

- sequential progression
- cyclic interaction
- recursive development

---

## Criterion 4 — Boundary

Observe transitions between organizational regions.

Typical observations include:

- layer transition
- functional migration
- boundary preservation

---

## Criterion 5 — Hierarchy

Observe organizational levels.

Typical examples include:

- local
- intermediate
- global
- cross-layer organization

---

# Scope

These criteria are intended for evaluating topology preservation across multiple
representation formats, including:

- documents
- workflow diagrams
- conceptual diagrams
- repository organization
- methodological documents

The criteria are independent of any particular domain and focus exclusively on
observable structural organization.
